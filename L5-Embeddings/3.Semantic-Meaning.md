# 1. How Do Embeddings Learn Meaning?

When an embedding layer is first created,

every embedding vector contains random numbers.

Example

```text
cat

↓

[0.32, -0.81, 0.15, ...]
```

These numbers have absolutely no meaning.

The model gradually learns meaningful vectors during training.

---

## The Learning Process

Every training step follows the same cycle.

```text
Sentence

↓

Tokenizer

↓

Token IDs

↓

Embedding Lookup

↓

Transformer

↓

Prediction

↓

Loss

↓

Backpropagation

↓

Update Embeddings
```

This process repeats billions of times.

Eventually,

the embeddings become meaningful.

---

# 2. What is a Forward Pass?

## Definition

A **Forward Pass** is the process where the model receives an input and predicts the next token.

Example

Input

```text
The capital of France is
```

The model predicts

```text
Paris
```

Initially,

the prediction is often incorrect because the embeddings are random.

---

# 3. What is Loss?

## Definition

Loss is a numerical value that measures how wrong the model's prediction is.

Lower loss means

the prediction is closer to the correct answer.

Higher loss means

the prediction is far from the correct answer.

---

## Example

Input

```text
The capital of France is
```

Correct answer

```text
Paris
```

Model predicts

```text
London
```

The loss becomes high.

The model now knows

something must change.

---

# 4. What is Backpropagation?

## Definition

Backpropagation is the process of determining how every trainable parameter contributed to the prediction error.

It calculates

"What should change to reduce the loss?"

Those changes include

- embedding vectors
- attention weights
- feed-forward weights

Everything learnable is updated.

---

## Real-Time Analogy

Imagine throwing a basketball.

If you miss,

you adjust

- angle
- force
- direction

You do not throw the ball exactly the same way again.

Training works similarly.

The model adjusts its parameters after every mistake.

---

# 5. How Are Embeddings Updated?

Suppose

```text
cat
```

has the embedding

```text
[0.31, -0.72, 0.51]
```

After one training step,

it becomes

```text
[0.33, -0.69, 0.56]
```

A tiny change.

After billions of updates,

the vector gradually represents meaningful relationships.

The model never assigns meaning manually.

It learns it from data.

---

# 6. Why Do Similar Words Become Similar?

Words appearing in similar contexts receive similar updates.

Example

```text
The cat is sleeping.

The dog is sleeping.
```

Both words appear

- after "The"
- before "is sleeping"

Because their contexts are similar,

their embeddings gradually move closer together.

---

Another example

```text
doctor

nurse

hospital

patient
```

These words frequently occur together.

Their embeddings become nearby in vector space.

---

# 7. What is Vector Space?

## Definition

Vector space is an abstract mathematical space where every embedding is represented as a point.

Instead of thinking about words,

imagine points.

```text
cat ●

dog ●

lion ●

car ●
```

Animals appear close together.

Vehicles appear elsewhere.

The model organizes words automatically during training.

---

# 8. Measuring Similarity

Once words become vectors,

we can compare them mathematically.

The two most common methods are

- Euclidean Distance
- Cosine Similarity

---

# 9. Euclidean Distance

## Definition

Euclidean Distance measures the straight-line distance between two vectors.

Smaller distance

↓

More similar

Larger distance

↓

Less similar

---

## Example

Imagine two cities.

Cities that are physically close

have a small distance.

Words work similarly.

---

## Limitation

Euclidean distance depends on magnitude.

Sometimes,

vectors have different lengths but similar meanings.

Therefore,

another metric is often preferred.

---

# 10. Cosine Similarity

## Definition

Cosine Similarity measures the angle between two vectors,

not their length.

If two vectors point in nearly the same direction,

their cosine similarity is close to 1.

---

## Example

```text
doctor

↓

Vector A
```

```text
physician

↓

Vector B
```

These vectors point in nearly the same direction.

Their cosine similarity is high.

---

Another example

```text
doctor

↓

Vector A
```

```text
banana

↓

Vector B
```

These vectors point in very different directions.

Their cosine similarity is low.

---

## Why Cosine Similarity?

Cosine similarity focuses on semantic direction,

not vector size.

This makes it very useful for

- semantic search
- recommendation systems
- retrieval systems
- embeddings

---

# 11. Static vs Contextual Embeddings

This is one of the biggest improvements introduced by Transformers.

---

## Static Embeddings

Older models like Word2Vec produce

one embedding per word.

Example

```text
bank
```

Always has

the same vector.

Whether the sentence is

```text
river bank
```

or

```text
bank account
```

the embedding remains identical.

---

## Problem

One word

Multiple meanings.

Static embeddings cannot distinguish them.

---

## Contextual Embeddings

Transformers generate embeddings

based on surrounding words.

Example

Sentence 1

```text
He deposited money in the bank.
```

Sentence 2

```text
The fisherman sat on the bank.
```

The word

```text
bank
```

receives different embeddings

because the surrounding context differs.

This is why Transformer embeddings are called **contextual embeddings**.

---

# 12. Why Are Contextual Embeddings Better?

They allow the model to understand

- ambiguity
- multiple meanings
- sentence context
- relationships between words

This significantly improves language understanding.

---

# Common Misconceptions

❌ Embeddings are manually assigned.

✅ They are learned automatically during training.

---

❌ Similar spellings produce similar embeddings.

✅ Similar contexts produce similar embeddings.

---

❌ Every word has only one embedding.

✅ Transformer models generate contextual embeddings that depend on surrounding words.

---

❌ Euclidean distance is always the best similarity metric.

✅ Cosine similarity is generally preferred for comparing embeddings.

---

# Summary

- Embeddings start as random vectors.
- The model predicts the next token using a forward pass.
- Loss measures prediction error.
- Backpropagation calculates how parameters should change.
- Embeddings are updated after every training step.
- Similar contexts lead to similar embeddings.
- Embeddings exist in a high-dimensional vector space.
- Cosine similarity measures semantic similarity between vectors.
- Modern Transformers generate contextual embeddings instead of one fixed embedding per word.
